If you can observe a craving without acting on it for 3 minutes, it will pass. Not because you're strong — because that's how the brain works.
What's actually happening in your brain
Dopamine anticipation spike. Your brain releases dopamine in anticipation of a reward — not after getting it. The craving IS the dopamine hit.
It's not about hunger. Cravings are memory + emotion + habit. They're triggered by cues — stress, boredom, a smell, a time of day.
You can interrupt the loop. Not by suppressing it — by observing it with curiosity instead of urgency.
The STOP method.
4 steps. Under 3 minutes. Works every time you use it.
S
Stop what you're doing
Put down your phone. Step away from the kitchen. Create 2 meters of physical distance from the trigger.
T
Take a breath
One slow breath in (4 sec) — one slow breath out (6 sec). This activates the parasympathetic system and interrupts the dopamine loop.
O
Observe with curiosity
Ask: "What triggered this? Hunger? Stress? Boredom?" Label the emotion without judging it. Labelling reduces its intensity by 30–50%.
P
Proceed — with a choice
Now you decide. Not the craving. You might still eat it — but it's a conscious decision, not a reaction. That distinction is everything.

MAINTAIN · When STOP isn't enough
5 craving redirects
that actually work.
Sometimes you need something to do with the energy. These aren't distractions — they're biological interrupts.
💧
Drink a large glass of water. Dehydration mimics hunger. Works for 30% of cravings.
🚶
Walk for 5 minutes. Movement breaks the craving circuit faster than willpower.
🫚
Eat a tablespoon of nut butter. Fat + protein = satiety signal that lasts 2 hours.
🫁
4-7-8 breathing. In 4 sec, hold 7, out 8. Activates vagus nerve. Craving drops within 2 minutes.
🍵
Hot herbal tea. The warmth + flavour activates the same oral satisfaction pathway as eating.
