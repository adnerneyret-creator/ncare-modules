Social interactions

Measurably.
Some people activate your stress response. Others calm it. This is biology, not preference.
What happens in your nervous system
Secure relationships activate the ventral vagal system — the social engagement branch of the parasympathetic nervous system. Cortisol drops. Oxytocin rises. You feel calm and safe.
Threatening or unpredictable relationships activate the sympathetic system. Cortisol rises. Your body treats social threat the same as physical threat — same hormones, same energy cost.
Draining relationships don't need to be hostile. A person who consistently takes without reciprocating creates a chronic low-grade stress response — even if every interaction seems fine on the surface.
You cannot maintain serenity while spending 80% of your social energy on people who deplete it. This is not selfish. It's physiological.
Your social energy audit.
Think of the 5–8 people you interact with most regularly. Split them honestly.
Recharges me
Drains me
Boundaries aren't walls.
They're dosage instructions.
You don't have to remove people. You have to manage how much energy you allocate.
Protect the rechargers
Schedule regular time with people who restore you — not when you're already depleted. These relationships need investment to stay strong. They're your recovery infrastructure.
Reduce the drainers
You don't need to end relationships. Shorter interactions. Less frequent. Less available. "I only have 20 minutes" is a complete sentence. So is "I can't talk right now."
Recover after draining interactions
Build in a transition ritual — 5-minute walk, breathing exercise, alone time — after interactions with draining people. You're not being dramatic. You're managing a real physiological cost.
💡 The hardest part: draining people are often people you love. Managing energy with them isn't about love — it's about sustainability. You can't give from empty.
SÉRÉNITÉ · Your social protocol
Your energy is finite.
Spend it like it matters.
The most common pattern in burnout: giving unlimited energy to people who take, while slowly withdrawing from people who restore. By the time you notice, the recharging relationships have faded.
Three changes. This week.
Schedule one thing with someone from your recharge list — not to catch up, but specifically because you need restoration.
Shorten one interaction with someone from your drain list — one conversation this week, 20 minutes shorter than usual.
Build one transition ritual — walk, breathe, or sit alone for 5 minutes after the next draining interaction. Notice what it does.
