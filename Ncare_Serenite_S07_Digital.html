Your phone is a
dopamine slot machine.
And the payout is unpredictable — which makes it impossible to stop.
Why you can't put it down
Variable reward schedule. Sometimes the scroll delivers something interesting, sometimes nothing. This unpredictability is the most addictive reward pattern known — same as slot machines.
Dopamine for anticipation, not reward. The spike happens when you reach for the phone — not when you find something good. You're addicted to the checking, not the content.
Cortisol amplification. Each notification triggers a micro stress response. 150+ checks per day × micro cortisol spike = chronic elevation you can't explain.

The one hour rule.
Two windows. Every day.
Not a digital detox. A structural boundary that actually works.
Window 1 — First hour of the day
No phone for 60 minutes after waking. You already know this from Module S04. The morning anchor needs a protected space. One hour is the minimum for cortisol rhythm to set properly.
Window 2 — Last hour before sleep
No screens for 60 minutes before bed. Blue light suppresses melatonin. But the bigger issue is cognitive — the brain needs deactivation time. Screens keep problem-solving circuits active when they need to wind down.
What to do instead
Paper book. Stretching. Conversation without phones on the table. Cooking. Drawing. Writing. Walking. The activity matters less than the absence of the variable reward loop.
⚡ The fastest win: charge your phone outside the bedroom tonight. One physical change that makes the rule automatic — no willpower required.
