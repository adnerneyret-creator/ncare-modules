Suppressing worry
makes it louder.
The ironic process theory. Trying not to think about something guarantees you think about it.
What doesn't work
"Stop worrying." Asking the brain to suppress a thought requires actively thinking about it to check if you're thinking about it. It amplifies the very thing you're trying to avoid.
Distraction. Scrolling, watching, eating to avoid the feeling. Works for minutes. The worry returns stronger when the distraction ends.
Rumination. Going over and over the same thought without structure. Keeps the amygdala activated without moving toward resolution.
What works
Scheduled worry time. Clinical research (Borkovec, 1983) shows that postponing worry to a specific, bounded time reduces total daily worry by 35–40%. The brain accepts the delay once it trusts the time is coming.
Writing it down. Transfers the worry from working memory (which keeps rehearsing it) to external storage. The brain releases its grip on thoughts it can see.
The three-step
worry window.
15 minutes. Same time every day. That's the container.
Step 1 — Schedule it (pick your time now)
Choose a time between 4pm and 7pm — early enough that cortisol drops before sleep, late enough to catch the day's worries. Same time every day. When a worry arrives outside the window: "Not now. 5:30pm."
Step 2 — Write everything down (15 minutes only)
All worries on paper. No filtering, no editing. Big fears, small irritations, everything. Once written, for each worry: is this in my control? Yes → write one action. No → acknowledge and let it sit.
Step 3 — Close the window (hard stop)
When the 15 minutes end — stop. Close the notebook. Do something physical immediately (walk, breathe, stretch). The physical transition signals to the brain that the worry session is complete.
📓 Paper is better than phone. Writing by hand engages different neural pathways than typing — it slows the process down and deepens the transfer from working memory.

With practice, your brain learns to wait. Worries that arise outside the window get a new response: "I'll deal with that at 5:30." And remarkably — most of them feel smaller when 5:30 arrives.
What your AI tracks
Your daily PSS-10 check-in captures whether the worry window is working. If stress scores don't drop after 2 weeks of consistent use — your AI will suggest an adapted approach. The method adjusts to you.
